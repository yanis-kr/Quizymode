## Wording and style rules learned

- Keep civics language neutral and nonpartisan.
- Favor roles, procedures, and structures over rhetoric.
- Use plain descriptions of institutions before introducing specialized terms.
- Prefer stable constitutional and institutional facts over current political disputes.
- Prefer official or clearly nonpartisan educational sources where practical.
- Ask about how systems work, what rights mean, and what institutions do.
- Keep explanations direct and civic-minded rather than argumentative.

## Weak patterns to avoid

- partisan or culture-war framing
- legal jargon without explanation
- unstable current-events questions without clear year anchoring
- vague “who has power?” stems that do not specify level or branch
- advocacy language presented as civics knowledge
- campaign-season facts that will age quickly
- distractors that are absurd or outside the topic domain
